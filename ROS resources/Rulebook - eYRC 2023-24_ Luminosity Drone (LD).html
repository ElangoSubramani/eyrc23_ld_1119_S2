1. Introduction

1.1 Background

Once upon a time, in the not-too-distant future, humans on Earth have reached new milestones in space exploration. Scientists have identified a nearby exoplanet that showed potential for supporting life, which led to the start of a mission of surveying the planet's surface and capturing any indications of organisms using its advanced thermal camera.

Luminosity Drone is no ordinary drone. Equipped with state-of-the-art technology and curious artificial intelligence, Luminosity Drone was designed to explore distant planets in search of signs of life.

As Luminosity Drone explored the alien landscape, it spotted a peculiar reading. The thermal camera detected a faint, fluctuating heat signature from a nearby cave. Luminosity Drone, driven by its programming and an inherent curiosity, glided following the source of these signals.

To its astonishment, Luminosity Drone realized that the heat signatures represented a diverse array of lifeforms. Each signature corresponded to a different organism, some larger and more complex, while others were smaller and simpler. Luminosity Drone had stumbled upon a hidden ecosystem, thriving in the planet's depths. Luminosity Drone continued its mission, exploring other planets and documenting the diverse thermal landscapes they possessed. With each new discovery, Luminosity Drone expanded humanity's understanding of the universe and deepened the collective curiosity about the mysteries beyond Earth.

And so, Luminosity Drone, the extraordinary drone, armed with its thermal camera and relentless in its pursuit of knowledge, has forever changed the course of humanity's understanding of life in the universe.

In the next section, we shall look at the detailed description of the Problem Statement of this theme!

1.2 Problem Statement

This theme’s problem statement consists of a Swift Drone (developed at e-Yantra) tasked with locating and identifying organisms (denoted by infrared LEDs) in their habitat on the surface of the exoplanet (denoted by Arena) using methodologies like image processing and path planning. Participants will first work on these tasks in simulation to hone their skills and finally learn to execute the same tasks on hardware.

1.3 Tools Used

To solve the problem statement Betaflight ecosystem is used for controlling the Swift Drone, Python for writing the autonomous stack, and ROS1 & ROS2 for integrating various aspects of autonomy required in the solution.

2. Theme Description

Luminosity drone theme is an abstraction of an exoplanet exploration drone. Swift Drone is responsible for the exploration of a given area on the surface of the exoplanet and the search for organisms (denoted by infrared LEDs) on the arena. After identifying/detecting the organisms, it needs to find the astrobio-coordinates (WhyCon coordinates) of the organism which will be the centroid of the LEDs in the cluster and the number of LEDs that represent the type of the organism. This is done using image processing and computer vision techniques. This entire task, from detection to reporting the astrobio-coordinates and type of organisms, has to be done as quickly as possible.

2.1 Terms

2.2.1 Swift Drone

The Swift Drone is an Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) developed at e-Yantra and is included in the kit provided to stage 2 participants, in the form factor of a quadcopter, the exploration vehicle in the theme. It has a WhyCon marker mounted on top of it for locating the Swift Drone easily and to serve as a feedback mechanism for the control system to control and move the drone. There is an infrared camera (night vision) attached to the drone which is used as a Surveillance Camera. The Swift Drone is controlled using an AIO flight controller along with Raspberry Pi as a companion computer for onboard processing.

2.2.2 LED Cluster

These are placed randomly on the arena and denote different organisms on the surface of the exoplanet. Each LED cluster will have to be detected by the Swift Drone and report on the given ROS topic. Each cluster consists of 2-5 LEDs, resistors, a switch, and a AAA battery all mounted on a perf board.

2.2.3 Whycon Marker

The WhyCon marker is made of an inner white patch surrounded by a black portion. It is attached to the top of the Swift drone for tracking the drone using the overhead camera.

2.2.4 Overhead Camera

An overhead camera with a fixed FOV is used to capture the arena for navigation of the drone. It is used to track the WhyCon marker on top of the drone to provide the position feedback for control of the drone.

2.3 Inputs

The input given to the participants consists of the coordinates of the starting point, the four organisms, and the landing point. All these coordinates will be given to the participants just before the theme run. This is to ensure the coordinates cannot be hardcoded into the script. The coordinates are of the form [row][column] printed on the arena, e.g. B4, C2. The LED clusters must be placed in the given location to prepare the arena. For placing the LED cluster on a particular coordinate, e.g. C4, participants can use the intersection of C row and 4th column as given on the arena. 4. Hardware and Software Specifications

4.1 Hardware Specifications

4.1.1. Swift Drone

The hardware to be used in the theme is provided in the kit. No changes in the Swift Drone are allowed in terms of hardware. Teams that need to replace any damaged component in the drone should get the approval from e-Yantra team.

4.1.2. LED clusters (Organisms)

Infrared LEDs and resistors to make the LED clusters cannot be changed by other LEDs or resistors.

4.2 Software Specifications

4.2.1. Ubuntu 20.04

The operating system on which all the software runs is Ubuntu 20.04.

4.2.2. ROS2 Foxy

Robot Operating System (ROS2) is the framework used to integrate the components in the theme. The version of ROS2 supported in the competition is Foxy

4.2.3. Python

  • Python is the language in which the participant’s programs are written

  • The version of Python supported with ROS2 Foxy is Python3.

  • Hence, all the Python programs interfacing with the ROS2 Foxy framework should be written in Python3. Python3 comes pre-installed with ROS2 Foxy.

5. Theme Flow

  • The arena has to be set up as per the given inputs.

  • The participants will be given a ros2 launch file that will start logging the necessary topics in the ros2 bag file.

  • All the scripts and programs must be started before the run starts.

  • The drone is armed at the starting point, indicating the start of the run. Time will be recorded from this point.

  • The Swift Drone surveys the entire arena looking for organisms. The drone should publish the WhyCon coordinates of the organism and the type of the organism onto the ROS2 topic ‘astrobiolocation’.

  • The Swift Drone also has to indicate the identification of organisms using an onboard buzzer and LEDs as given in section 6.3

  • The Swift Drone has to detect and report the location of as many organisms as possible in a 250-second timeframe.

  • The drone lands at the landing point to indicate the end of the run.

6. Theme Rules

  • The run will have a finite duration, with a maximum time limit of 250 seconds.

  • To signal the start of the run, the Swift Drone needs to beep a one-second beep from the buzzer. The beginning of the run is considered either from the buzzer beep or the takeoff of the Swift Drone, whichever occurs first. The end of the run is indicated by a two-second beep from the buzzer. If the buzzer does not beep, the end of the run will be considered at time = 250 seconds (full time).

  • A maximum of 4 organisms will be present in the arena. The Swift Drone must detect as many organisms as possible, report the WhyCon coordinates of the organisms, and land back in the designated area within the specified timeframe.

  • Every team will be given 2 attempts for the entire theme run. The best of the two will be considered.

6.1. Takeoff / Landing Rules

  • The Swift Drone should be positioned at the start point coordinates specified for each theme run. The WhyCon coordinates of the start point will be captured at the beginning of the run, indicating the WhyCon coordinates of the takeoff.

  • The Swift Drone should land at the landing point coordinates specified for each theme run. The WhyCon coordinates of the landing point will be captured at the conclusion of the run, indicating the WhyCon coordinates of the landing.

6.2. Detection Rules

  • When the Swift drone detects an organism using its onboard infrared camera, it has to send the arena coordinate at which the organism is found, for eg. A2, E5, etc

6.3. Identification Rules

  • The different organism types according to the no. of LEDs are given below:

6.4. Repositioning Rules

  • Once the Swift Drone is armed, no manual intervention is allowed.

  • If the Swift Drone crashes on the ground or into any structure and is no longer controllable by the algorithm, then it will indicate the end of the run and the maximum time will be considered to calculate the score. However, if after any kind of crash, the drone can still continue to operate without any manual intervention, then the run can continue.

Note : No strings will be attached to the drone.

7. Judging and Scoring Rules

The team’s total score is calculated by the following formula:

Total Score = (250-T) + (OD * 100) + (OI * 100) + LB + Bonus

T: Maximum time is 250 seconds (4:10 minutes)

Start of the run: The start of the run is indicated by a single buzzer beep of 1 sec or takeoff whichever is earlier

End of the run: The end of the run is indicated by a single buzzer beep of 2 sec.

LB: Landing bonus. Awarded when a successful landing is done at the landing point. LB has 100 points

OD (0-4): OD is the number of successful organism detections. To report an organism successfully, the location of the organism is to be published on a ros2 topic.

OI (0-4): OI is the number of organisms identified successfully. To identify an organism successfully, the correct organism type has to be reported in the same ros2 message while reporting a detection. Successful OI also includes the beeping of a buzzer and LED for the number of times equal to the organism type. For eg. organism type = alien_a, beep buzzer and LED on the Swift drone 2 times

Bonus: A Bonus of 200 points is awarded if all the organisms placed on the arena are detected and identified correctly with <250s time taken and the landing bonus is also awarded

Valid Run: One correct OD needs to be done for a valid run

Notes:

  • At least one correct detection is required for a valid theme run even if the identification of the organism is wrong.

  • The bonus mentioned in the scoring formula above is worth 200 points and is granted only when the drone is able to take off, detect all the organisms correctly report the location as well as identify all the organisms correctly, and land in the designated area.

  • If the drone malfunctions/crashes / goes out of the camera frame and is not able to come back into it, it is still considered an attempt.

  • An edge case is when the drone takes off, detects, and reports the location of all the organisms in the arena, but crashes shortly after. Even in this scenario, the end time would be considered 250 since the drone never landed, hence assumed to land at time infinity, which is 250.
